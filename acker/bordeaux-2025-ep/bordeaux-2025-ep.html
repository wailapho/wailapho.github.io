Introduction

A really good year it is. A great year everywhere, it is not.

Returning from four days in Bordeaux, and after tasting nearly 150 wines, we are happy to conclude that 2025 is a return to form for Bordeaux after last year's washout. The highs are higher than 2023, in some cases even meriting near-perfect scores, and there are over 40 wines we will be very happy to recommend.

That is not to say this is a year to buy across the board, far from it, but there are bound to be classical, delicious wines that might well in years to come look like savvy purchases.

We hope you enjoy our report below, and do let us know if you would like to register your interest.

Either way, we are unlikely to see such quality at these prices again.

The Vintage in Five Words

Small, Classical, Delicious, Paradoxical, and potentially Quixotic.

Small
but perfectly formed

Mostly because it was a very dry year throughout the Bordeaux appellations. Late-August thunderstorms saved the crop from total desiccation, but most properties had tiny berries weighing a gram at best. Poor flower-initiation from the previous year meant that there were fewer grapes per bunch too. Thus the average yield is less than 30 hectolitres per hectare versus 48 hl/ha, with volumes half of what 2016 was — the smallest crop since 1991. Cheval Blanc cropped at 15 hl/ha.

Classical
'96 / '16

Stylistically, the wines look back to the more classical, structured vintages — '96 and '16 spring to mind. The fruit is fresh, the acidity bright, and the tannins are present without being aggressive. These are wines of architecture rather than opulence, of line and length rather than sheer pomp. For the long-term collector, that's no bad thing.

Delicious
Ripe tannins

Courtesy of the sun, nearly every wine we tasted had ripe tannins. This, coupled with the fresh acidity and more elegant fruit profiles, gives wines a lovely succulence and drinkability, with very few unbalanced or disjointed wines. They will probably also be fairly approachable when young, much like the 2023s.

Paradoxical
12.5–13.5%

With climatic parallels to 2003 or 2022, one would expect alcohol levels in the 14.5%+ range, but most are in the low teens — 12.5–13.5%. Similarly with the flavour profile, which is much more Atlantic and cool than some of the more sunny vintages like 2015 or 2018 — a "solar" vintage this is not. Lastly, warm and dry vintages normally favour Bordeaux all over and lend to homogenously good wines, like 2009 or 2020; frustratingly, not every property has produced a great wine and there is far more appellational variation.

Quixotic
either / or

In the idealistic, romantic and possibly unrealistic sense — the Châteaux have once again produced delicious, classical wines at a time that prices in the secondary market have been falling, and with a deteriorating global macro-economic picture. All of which means that despite their best efforts, this will be a campaign to navigate carefully. The upshot for the collector is that the 2025s will likely be released in line or just above their 2024 predecessors; most Châteaux we spoke to were aware of the broader market picture, the lack of demand in the previous year, and wanting to make a success of this one.

An Inflection Point

With En Primeur and Bordeaux as a whole at an inflection point, one of two things will happen.

Either

The campaign will be a success, where Châteaux honour last year's historic low prices, and everyone will comment in the coming years how 2025 was the last "affordable vintage" — and "we should have bought more".

OR
Or

The campaign will meet a potentially recalcitrant market — lack of consumer demand, misjudged pricing — and be deemed a failure with poor sales along the chain, forcing the Châteaux to cut production costs and thus making a lesser-quality wine going forward.

Either way, we are unlikely to see such quality at these prices again.

"So what?" the price-burnt historic En Primeur buyer might say — and they'd be right not to leap in. We would certainly advise caution; hence the small, separate recommendation lists below. However, if you're reading this, you're certainly interested in Bordeaux — and there are wines this year that are definitely worthy of your attention and your cellar.

Lastly, En Primeur is a great time to buy in formats other than bottles. Post-bottling, halves, magnums, double-magnums and larger are often harder to track down or carry a premium. We will not be charging anything more than the per-bottle price this year.

Appellation Summary

This is neither a Left-bank nor a Right-bank favoured vintage. The best appellation as a whole on the Left is Margaux, with strong showings across the range, and St-Émilion on the Right. That said, Pauillac beyond the Firsts and Seconds underperformed — a similar story over in Pomerol. St-Estèphe and St-Julien both have flashes of greatness in the usual places, whilst the Haut-Médoc and Graves have some excellent wines from familiar names.
